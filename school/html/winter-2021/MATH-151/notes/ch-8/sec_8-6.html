Section 8.6: Integration Strategies

Recognize A Common Pattern Present in the Integrand

  1. Integrating the product of two functions often calls for integration by parts (Section 8.2), particularly if the factors of the product are a polynomial and a transcendental function (a logarithmic, exponential, trigonometric, or inverse trigonometric function). In some cases, integegration by parts is successful when the integrand is a single function (for example, \(\int \ln(x)\;dx\) or \(\int \sin^{-1}(x)\;dx\))
  2. Powers and products or \(\sin(x)\) and \(\cos(x)\), \(\tan(x)\) and \(\sec(x)\), or \(\cot(x)\) and \(\csc(x)\) are integrated using identities, reduction formulas, or the substitutions given in Tables 8.2 and 8.3 of ðŸ“‘ Section 8.3.
  3. Integrals containing the expression \(a^{2} - x^{2}, a^{2} + x^{2}\), or \(x^{2} - a^{2}\) may yield to trigonometric substitution.
